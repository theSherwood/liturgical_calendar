## Meaning

A young tradition, invented by the rationalist community, for people who want the meaning of a solstice ritual without the supernatural. Its shape is honest: it begins in full light and songs of celebration, moves down into darkness and unflinching songs about how big and cold the universe is and how alone we might be — and then, at the bottom, chooses hope anyway. It's a ceremony about facing reality squarely and still deciding to carry the light forward.

## Observance

Gather with candles as the sun sets on the longest night. Start bright and warm. Then, one by one, put out the candles until you're in the dark together, and speak plainly about what's hard and frightening — in the world and close to home. Then, from a single flame, relight them, and sing or speak about what's worth protecting. End in warmth, having earned it.

## Reading

> Our posturings, our imagined self-importance, the delusion that we have some privileged position in the Universe, are challenged by this point of pale light. Our planet is a lonely speck in the great enveloping cosmic dark. In our obscurity, in all this vastness, there is no hint that help will come from elsewhere to save us from ourselves. … To me, it underscores our responsibility to deal more kindly with one another, and to preserve and cherish the pale blue dot, the only home we've ever known.
> — Carl Sagan, Pale Blue Dot: A Vision of the Human Future in Space (1994)
