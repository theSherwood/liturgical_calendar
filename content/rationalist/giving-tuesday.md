## Meaning

The calendar's turn from gratitude to generosity. After Thanksgiving's feast and the shopping days that follow it, one day set aside for giving — and, in our family, for giving *well*. Not all help helps equally; some gifts do a hundred times the good of others for the same cost. Taking that seriously is its own kind of love.

## Observance

As a family, choose somewhere to give and do it together today. Look up how much good the gift actually does — lives improved per dollar, not warm feelings per dollar. Let the children pick a share. Make generosity a thing we practice on purpose, not just feel.

## Reading

> If I am walking past a shallow pond and see a child drowning in it, I ought to wade in and pull the child out. This will mean getting my clothes muddy, but this is insignificant, while the death of the child would presumably be a very bad thing. … For the principle takes, firstly, no account of proximity or distance. It makes no moral difference whether the person I can help is a neighbor's child ten yards from me or a Bengali whose name I shall never know, ten thousand miles away.
> — Peter Singer, "Famine, Affluence, and Morality" (Philosophy & Public Affairs, 1972)
