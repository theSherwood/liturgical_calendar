## Meaning

Wep Ronpet means the Opening of the Year, and the ancient Egyptians did not choose its date by decree but read it off the sky. Each summer, after weeks of absence, the star Sirius rose again just before dawn, and within days the Nile began its life-giving flood. The Egyptians made a goddess of that star, Sopdet, and treated her reappearance as the world renewing its promise. It is one of humanity's oldest New Years, and one of the purest: a civilization organizing its entire calendar around a single star and a rising river.

## Observance

Wake before dawn and go outside to watch the eastern sky lighten, the way the priests once watched for Sopdet. Talk about what the family wants the new year to carry away and what it should bring in, like the flood that scoured the fields and left them richer. Then share a meal built around bread and water, the two things the Nile made possible.

## Reading

The Egyptians left temple calendars and hymns, but we could not confirm the exact wording of an ancient line about Sopdet against a reliable source, so we leave the star to speak plainly. For thousands of years the whole rhythm of Egyptian life turned on one observation: that Sirius, gone for seventy days, would climb back into the dawn and the river would follow. To open the year this way is to remember that our ancestors trusted the sky enough to build a civilization on its schedule.
