## Meaning

The Romans gave the year's first day to Janus — the two-faced god of doorways, who looks backward and forward at once. It's the most honest image of a new year we have: you can't step through a door without seeing where you came from and where you're going in the same glance.

## Observance

A Janus reflection to pair with New Year's Day: each person says one thing they see looking back at the old year, and one thing they see looking ahead. The Romans exchanged figs, honey, and good words for luck — do the same.

## Reading

> "The ancients called me Chaos, for a being from of old am I."
> — Ovid, Fasti (Janus speaking)

The Romans gave the year's first day to Janus, the two-faced god of doorways, who looks backward and forward in the same instant. Ovid, in the poem he wrote about the Roman calendar itself, lets the god speak: older than the gods, he is the hinge on which beginnings turn. It is the truest image of a new year we have — you cannot pass through a door without seeing, at once, the room you are leaving and the room you are entering.
