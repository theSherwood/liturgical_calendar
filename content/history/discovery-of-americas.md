## Meaning

On 12 October 1492 two halves of the human world, separated for millennia, made permanent contact — and nothing was ever the same for either. It was one of the most consequential days in all of history: it remade global trade, food, language, and the map, and it set the modern world in motion. It was also a catastrophe. For the peoples already living in the Americas, it began centuries of conquest, enslavement, and disease that killed the overwhelming majority of them. We mark the day honestly, holding both truths at once, because history is not a story of heroes and it is not owed our applause.

## Observance

A day for looking at history without flinching. Eat something from the great exchange of foods (the potato, tomato, chocolate, and chili all crossed the ocean because of this). Learn the name of one people who lived in the Americas before 1492 and what became of them. Practice holding a hard, mixed truth without collapsing it into a simple story.

## Reading

> "They would make fine servants… With fifty men we could subjugate them all and make them do whatever we want."
> — Christopher Columbus, journal, 1492

He wrote that of people who had swum out to greet him with gifts. The world he opened was real and world-changing; so was the price, paid by others. Honor the truth, not the legend.
