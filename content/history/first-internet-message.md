## Meaning

On the night of 29 October 1969, a student at UCLA tried to log in to a computer at Stanford Research Institute over a brand-new network called ARPANET. He typed an L, then an O, and the system promptly crashed. So the first message ever sent between two computers was simply "lo" — a stammered fragment of "login" that happened to sound like the opening of an epic. The whole internet, every message and argument and act of kindness since, grew out of that broken word. It is a good reminder that world-changing things often begin clumsily.

## Observance

Send someone a deliberately tiny message today — just "lo" — and let them ask what it means. Talk about how the network that now carries the whole family's lives began as two machines failing to say hello. Marvel a little that any of it works at all.

## Reading

> They succeeded in transmitting the "l" and the "o" and then the system crashed! Hence, the first message on the Internet was "lo", as in "lo and behold!"
> — Leonard Kleinrock, on the first ARPANET transmission

Kleinrock led the UCLA team that sent it; the full login went through about an hour later.
