## Meaning

On a cold, windy morning at Kitty Hawk, North Carolina, on December 17, 1903, Orville Wright lifted off the sand and flew about 120 feet in twelve seconds, the first sustained, controlled, powered flight by a human being. They flew four times that day, the longest a whole 59 seconds, and then walked to a telegraph office to tell their father. Within a single lifetime, that twelve-second hop grew into transatlantic airliners and, eventually, into the rockets that reached the Moon. It is one of the clearest turning points there is: the exact morning humanity stopped being a creature bound to the ground.

## Observance

Go somewhere open and cold, the way Kitty Hawk was, and fly something, a paper airplane, a kite, a cheap balsa glider. Read the Wright brothers' telegram aloud and marvel that the whole age of flight began with a message worrying about getting home for Christmas. Talk about how the biggest changes often look small and ridiculous on the first day.

## Reading

> Success four flights thursday morning all against twenty one mile wind started from level with engine power alone average speed through air thirty one miles longest 57 seconds inform Press home Christmas.
> — Orville Wright, telegram to Bishop Milton Wright, December 17, 1903

The telegrapher garbled it a little: the longest flight was really 59 seconds, and Orville's own name came through misspelled as "Orevelle." Even the announcement of the air age was an ordinary, slightly imperfect message about getting home for the holidays.
