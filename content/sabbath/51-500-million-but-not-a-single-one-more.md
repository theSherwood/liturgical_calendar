## Meaning

After weeks of arguing about value and the far future in the abstract, this piece is the proof that the project is real. Smallpox killed an almost unthinkable number of people across human history, and then, within living memory, coordinated human effort tracked it down case by case and drove it out of existence forever. The essay reads the eradication as a kind of eulogy and a battle hymn at once, honoring the dead while insisting that the enemy is not merely defeated but gone. It is what Moloch's opposite looks like: people cooperating across every border to make a permanent gift to everyone who will ever live, the not-yet-born who will simply never have to fear this thing.

## Observance

Read it together, and let it be the season's note of earned hope. Then name one other harm you would want humanity to make extinct, not managed or reduced but ended, so that no future child ever meets it. Talk about what it would take, and who would have to cooperate, to make that real.

## Reading

The essay tells the story of smallpox as both a memorial for its hundreds of millions of victims and a triumph, closing on the fact that after 1977 the disease killed no one else, ever. Read it aloud together; it is written to be felt, not summarized. Then look at the eradication record and notice that this is a thing our species actually did.
