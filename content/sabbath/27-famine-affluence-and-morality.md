## Meaning

This is the essay that opens the season, and the one everything else answers to. Singer's argument is almost embarrassingly simple: if it is in your power to prevent something bad without giving up anything of comparable importance, you ought to do it. He makes it vivid with a child drowning in a shallow pond — you would ruin your shoes to save her without a second thought — and then points out that distance and numbers do not actually change the math, only how it feels. The discomfort you feel reading it is the whole point; it means the argument landed.

## Observance

Read it together and sit with the pond. Then ask, plainly and without anyone having to answer out loud: what is the muddy-clothes cost you keep declining to pay, and is it really of comparable moral importance to what is at stake? Let the question be uncomfortable for a minute before you move on with your day.

## Reading

Singer's drowning-child thought experiment is best met in his own framing rather than paraphrased here: a child is drowning in a shallow pond you are walking past, and wading in will cost you only muddy clothes. His claim is that if you would obviously save her, then the same obligation follows whenever you can prevent something very bad at small cost to yourself — near or far, one child or thousands.
