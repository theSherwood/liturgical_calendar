## Meaning

Scott Alexander gives a name to a move we all pull without noticing: suddenly demanding airtight proof, but only for the claims we already wanted to reject. Skepticism is a virtue applied evenly; it becomes a trick when you unleash it on your opponent's evidence and quietly wave your own through. The essay's running example is Heraclitus, who insists on impossible philosophical rigor exactly where it happens to benefit him. The unsettling part is how reasonable each isolated demand feels in the moment — which is why the defense is not lower standards but consistent ones.

## Observance

This week, watch for the phrase "but how do you really know" — in others and in yourself. When it appears, ask whether you would accept that same level of doubt aimed at a belief you like. Try holding both sides of a disagreement to the exact same bar and see which claims survive.

## Reading

In the essay, Heraclitus wields philosophical skepticism as a one-way weapon, applying it only where it wins him something.

> Philosophical rigor, usually a virtue, has been debased to an isolated demand for rigor in cases where it benefits Heraclitus.
> — Scott Alexander, "Beware Isolated Demands for Rigor"
