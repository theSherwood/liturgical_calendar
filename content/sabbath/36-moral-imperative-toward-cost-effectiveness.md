## Meaning

Ord takes the dry phrase "cost-effectiveness" and shows the lives hidden inside it. In global health, the best interventions are not marginally better than the typical ones — they can be tens or hundreds of times better, spread across orders of magnitude. When that is true, moving a fixed budget from a mediocre program to an excellent one saves nearly as many people as finding entirely new money would. Ignoring the spread is therefore not neutral or modest; it quietly costs real lives that a moment's attention could have saved. The imperative in the title is meant literally.

## Observance

Read it together and let the scale land: not twice as good, but a hundred times. Talk about where else in life you would happily accept a hundred-fold worse result out of habit or squeamishness — and why giving might deserve the same scrutiny you give a big purchase. Sit with the idea that comparing carefully is itself a form of caring.

## Reading

> However, when one option is 10 times or 100 times better, as is often the case in global health, redirecting funding is so important that it is almost as good as adding new funding directly towards the superior intervention.
> — Toby Ord, "The Moral Imperative Toward Cost-Effectiveness in Global Health"
