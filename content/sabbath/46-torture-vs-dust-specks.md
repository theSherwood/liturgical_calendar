## Meaning

This is the season's most uncomfortable reading, and it earns its place precisely because it refuses to let you off easy. Yudkowsky poses a choice between one person suffering fifty years of torture and an almost incomprehensibly vast number of people each getting a single dust speck in the eye, then argues that if you take aggregation seriously, the sheer size of the second number has to matter. Most people's stomachs revolt, and that revolt is worth examining rather than trusting. The reading is not really about specks; it is about whether our moral intuitions can be trusted at scales they never evolved to feel, which is exactly the scale the far future asks us to think at.

## Observance

Read it together, then let everyone react honestly, including the gut horror. Talk about where you think the intuition and the arithmetic come apart, and whether it is the numbers you distrust or your own feelings. You do not have to reach agreement; sitting in the discomfort together is the practice.

## Reading

> Would you prefer that one person be horribly tortured for fifty years without hope or rest, or that 3^^^3 people get dust specks in their eyes? I think the answer is obvious.
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Torture vs. Dust Specks"
