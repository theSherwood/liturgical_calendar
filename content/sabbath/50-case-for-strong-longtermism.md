## Meaning

Greaves and MacAskill make the argument the season has been building toward, carefully and in the open. Their claim is that for a wide range of decisions, the best thing you can do is judged mostly by its effects on the very long-run future, because the number of people who might live in that future is so vast that even small, reliable improvements to their prospects can outweigh large benefits available today. It is the philosophers' version of the intuition behind Astronomical Waste, laid out with its objections faced head on. Whether or not you end up fully convinced, wrestling with it changes how you weigh the present against the enormous, silent constituency of the not-yet-born.

## Observance

Read the introduction together, or the whole paper if you are feeling ambitious. Then have the honest argument: does the future's size really make its people count for more, or is there something it leaves out? Try steelmanning the side you dislike. The point this week is to take the strongest version of the claim seriously before you accept or reject it.

## Reading

Rather than quote the paper out of context, read its opening pages together. Its thesis is bracing in one line: for many of the choices we face, what matters most about an action is its effect on the far future, because the sheer number of lives to come can outweigh nearer, more visible goods. Whether that lands as obvious or outrageous is exactly the discussion worth having.
