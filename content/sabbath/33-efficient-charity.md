## Meaning

Scott Alexander's move here is to take a rule everyone claims to endorse — love your neighbor as yourself — and hold you to it. When you spend on yourself for something that matters, you compare options ruthlessly; you would not buy a pretty picture instead of a warm parka for an Arctic expedition. Charity, he argues, deserves the same seriousness, because the differences between good and great giving are not small — they are the difference between one life saved and ten. Treating your neighbor as yourself means being as careful with their welfare as with your own, which turns out to be a surprisingly demanding standard.

## Observance

Read it together and run the polar-trek test on one real donation, past or planned: if this were your own survival budget, would you still spend it here? Then look up where a similar amount does the most measurable good and sit with the gap. The exercise is not guilt; it is respect for the people the money is actually for.

## Reading

> If you are to "love your neighbor as yourself", then you should be as careful in maximizing the benefit to others when donating to charity as you would be in maximizing the benefit to yourself when choosing purchases for a polar trek.
> — Scott Alexander, "Efficient Charity: Do Unto Others"
