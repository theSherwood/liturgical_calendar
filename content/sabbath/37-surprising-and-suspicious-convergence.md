## Meaning

Lewis names a very human trick of the mind. It is convenient when the cause you already favor turns out to be the best answer to every question at once — best for the arts and for human welfare, best for the near term and the far future. But the world is not usually so tidy: what is best for one goal is rarely also best for an unrelated one, so when your favorite keeps winning every race, the likeliest explanation is not luck but motivated reasoning. The essay is a small, useful alarm bell to keep near your convictions, especially the ones you would least like to be wrong about.

## Observance

Read it together, then each name a belief where your preferred option conveniently wins on multiple counts. Ask, out loud and without defensiveness, whether that convergence is real or whether you reasoned backward from what you already wanted. Ringing the bell on yourself is the whole ritual.

## Reading

> — Gregory Lewis, "Beware Surprising and Suspicious Convergence"
