## Meaning

Scott Alexander takes apart a comforting story we tell about ourselves: that being tolerant means loving people far away and unlike us. He argues that tolerating the distant and exotic costs nothing; the real test is the group right next to you, the one you define yourself against. Your outgroup is rarely some faraway enemy — it is the neighbors, the relatives, the near-cousins whose small differences drive you up the wall. For a family that has left one tribe and drifted toward another, this is bracing: the tribe you scorn most is the measure of the tribe you have joined.

## Observance

Each person names the group they find it hardest to be charitable toward — and notices how close to home it actually sits. Then try, out loud, to state that group's best sincere argument as they would state it, with no sarcasm. Charity toward the outgroup is the muscle this reading asks you to exercise.

## Reading

> If you're part of the Blue Tribe, then your outgroup isn't al-Qaeda, or Muslims, or blacks, or gays, or transpeople, or Jews, or atheists – it's the Red Tribe.
> — Scott Alexander, "I Can Tolerate Anything Except the Outgroup"
