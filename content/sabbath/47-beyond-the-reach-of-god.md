## Meaning

For a family with a religious past, this essay names something quietly load-bearing: the felt assumption, lingering long after belief has gone, that the universe will not let things get truly, unfixably bad. Yudkowsky asks you to actually give that up, to inhabit a world with no author ensuring a happy ending, where horror can arrive for no reason at all. It is bleak on its surface and bracing underneath, because the same fact that removes the guarantee also hands us the responsibility. If no one is keeping the world safe, then whether it is kept safe is up to us.

## Observance

Read it together, and let it be a solemn one. Then ask, gently, where each of you still secretly expects the universe to catch you, some leftover sense that it will all work out on its own. Naming that reflex is the first step to acting as though the outcome depends on us, because it does.

## Reading

> The main reason to doubt would be refusal to accept that the universe could make so little sense—that horrible things could happen so lightly, for no more reason than a roll of the dice.
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Beyond the Reach of God"
