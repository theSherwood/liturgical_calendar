## Meaning

Elmore's insight is that triage is not a grim exception we occasionally fall into; it is the permanent condition of a world with limited resources and unlimited need. Every dollar and hour spent one place is one not spent another, so we are always, whether we admit it or not, deciding who gets helped. The comforting idea that refusing to prioritize keeps our hands clean is an illusion — inaction allocates too, just blindly. Facing triage honestly is not playing God; it is the opposite of pretending the choices are not ours.

## Observance

Read it together and drop the fantasy of the neutral option for a minute. Name one place your family's resources already flow by default, and acknowledge that this is a triage decision, quietly made. The practice is not to feel crushed by it but to make one such choice on purpose this week instead of by drift.

## Reading

> Making better choices through conscious triage is no more "playing God" than blithely abdicating responsibility for the effects of our actions. Both choices are choices to let some live and others die. The only difference is that the person who embraces triage has a chance to use their brain to improve the outcome.
> — Holly Elmore, "We Are In Triage Every Second Of Every Day"
