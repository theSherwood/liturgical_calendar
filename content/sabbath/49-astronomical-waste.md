## Meaning

Bostrom's short paper does something dizzying with arithmetic. If the future could hold vast numbers of worthwhile lives among the stars, then the resources burning unused every second, the potential lives not being lived, represent a loss on a scale the mind cannot hold. From that he draws a startling conclusion: for anyone who cares about aggregate good, the overwhelming priority is not to speed the future up but to make sure we get there at all, to reduce the risk of never arriving. It reframes patience and safety as the most urgent values of all, because the prize is so large that protecting the path to it dwarfs almost everything else.

## Observance

Read it together, then sit for a moment with the biggest number in the piece and try, and fail, to actually feel it. Talk about what changes if you take it seriously: does it make today feel small, or does it make guarding the future feel enormous? Notice which way it moves each of you.

## Reading

> the potential for approximately 10^38 human lives is lost every century that colonization of our local supercluster is delayed; or equivalently, about 10^29 potential human lives per second.
> — Nick Bostrom, "Astronomical Waste"
