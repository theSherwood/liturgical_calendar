## Meaning

This is the humble counterweight to a season spent sharpening our minds. Scott Alexander confesses that he often cannot beat a well-made argument for something obviously false — and that the right response is not to be convinced, but to shrug and keep his prior. If a talented arguer can make any position sound airtight, then how convincing an argument feels tells you almost nothing about whether it is true. Refusing to be swept away by cleverness is not closed-mindedness; it is knowing the limits of your own in-the-moment reasoning. It is the difference between a supple mind and a suggestible one.

## Observance

Recall a time someone talked you into something you later realized was wrong. What made it persuasive, and what should have tipped you off? Agree together on one topic where you will deliberately trust your background knowledge over any single slick argument until you have had time to check it.

## Reading

> This is the correct Bayesian action: if I know that a false argument sounds just as convincing as a true argument, argument convincingness provides no evidence either way. I should ignore it and stick with my prior.
> — Scott Alexander, "Epistemic Learned Helplessness"
