## Meaning

People love to say "absence of evidence is not evidence of absence," and in strict logic they have half a point — failing to prove something is not the same as disproving it. But probability is subtler and more honest: if a claim predicts you would probably see some sign, then not seeing that sign should lower your confidence, even if only a little. Every observation that a true belief would have made likely, and a false one unlikely, is evidence — and that runs in both directions. This is one of those small hinges on which clear thinking turns. Once you feel it, you cannot un-feel it.

## Observance

Read it aloud, then find a claim where "we haven't seen it, so who knows" is doing the work. Ask together: if this were true, would we probably have noticed by now? Let the honest answer nudge your confidence up or down.

## Reading

> Absence of proof is not proof of absence. … But in probability theory, absence of evidence is always evidence of absence.
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Absence of Evidence Is Evidence of Absence"
