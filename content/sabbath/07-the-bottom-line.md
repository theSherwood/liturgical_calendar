## Meaning

Picture a clever arguer who writes the conclusion he wants at the bottom of the page, then fills in reasons above it. However brilliant those reasons, the bottom line was fixed before the thinking started — so it carries no information about the truth. The lesson is uncomfortable and clarifying: what makes your conclusions trustworthy is the process that actually produced them, not the justifications you can muster afterward. Most bad reasoning is not stupid; it is a bottom line written too early. Watching for the order in which your own conclusions arrive is a quiet, powerful discipline.

## Observance

Read it together, then each recall a recent decision and ask honestly: did the reasons come first, or the conclusion? No shame either way — the point is to feel the difference between reasoning toward an answer and reasoning from one.

## Reading

> Your effectiveness as a rationalist is determined by whichever algorithm actually writes the bottom line of your thoughts.
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky, "The Bottom Line"
