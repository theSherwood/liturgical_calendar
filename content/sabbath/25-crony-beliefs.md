## Meaning

Kevin Simler offers a wonderfully useful metaphor: your beliefs are like employees, and they come in two kinds. Merit beliefs earn their keep by being accurate — they help you navigate the real world, so you hire them for the job they do. Crony beliefs are hired for a different reason: they win you friends, status, and belonging, whether or not they are true, like the boss's useless nephew kept on the payroll for who he knows. The trouble is that crony beliefs disguise themselves as merit beliefs, and we defend them hardest precisely because their real job is social. Escaping a tribe often means quietly laying off a few cronies.

## Observance

Ask, gently and without accusation, whether any belief in the household might be on the payroll for social reasons — because it marks you as part of the right crowd. You do not have to fire it tonight. Just notice which beliefs you would find embarrassing to drop in front of your friends, and wonder why.

## Reading

> Both contribute to our bottom line — survival and reproduction — but they do so in different ways: merit beliefs by helping us navigate the world, crony beliefs by helping us look good.
> — Kevin Simler, "Crony Beliefs"
