## Meaning

You can hold a belief on one level and, underneath, anticipate the opposite — and the tell is that you already know exactly which tests you'll need to excuse. Carl Sagan's invisible, inaudible, flour-permeable dragon is the picture of it: the claimant has an accurate model of the world hidden away, because they can predict, in advance, precisely where the dragon must not be found. It's a kind, human failing, and worth watching for in ourselves before others.

## Observance

Read the dragon parable aloud. Then ask, without teeth: is there anything we say we believe but don't actually plan our lives around? No confessions required — just the noticing.

## Reading

> Carl Sagan once told a parable of someone who comes to us and claims: "There is a dragon in my garage." … We propose to toss a bag of flour into the air to see if it outlines an invisible dragon, and the claimant immediately says, "The dragon is permeable to flour." … The claimant must have an accurate model of the situation somewhere in their mind, because they can anticipate, in advance, exactly which experimental results they'll need to excuse.
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Belief in Belief"
