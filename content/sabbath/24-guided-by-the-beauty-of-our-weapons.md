## Meaning

After a hard look at how tribes and politics wreck our thinking, this reading offers a reason for hope. Scott Alexander distinguishes symmetric weapons, which help whoever wields them best regardless of who is right, from asymmetric ones, which favor the truth. Violence and raw rhetoric are symmetric — a liar can shout or fight as well as anyone. But honest logical debate, slow and unglamorous as it is, works a little better for the side that happens to be correct, because in the long run true claims have more real evidence behind them. That small asymmetry is the whole case for arguing in good faith instead of just trying to win.

## Observance

Have a disagreement at the table on purpose, and pick your weapons deliberately: no volume, no mockery, no motives-questioning — only reasons, evidence, and honest questions. Notice how much harder and slower it is, and whether the better-supported view starts to show through anyway.

## Reading

> Logical debate has one advantage over narrative, rhetoric, and violence: it's an asymmetric weapon. That is, it's a weapon which is stronger in the hands of the good guys than in the hands of the bad guys.
> — Scott Alexander, "Guided by the Beauty of Our Weapons"
