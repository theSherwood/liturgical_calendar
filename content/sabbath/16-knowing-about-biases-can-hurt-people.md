## Meaning

There is a cruel twist in learning about human irrationality: the list of biases makes a wonderful arsenal for shooting down other people. Once you know the words — motivated reasoning, confirmation bias, the halo effect — you can wave them at any argument you would rather not accept, and feel clever while doing it. The reading warns that a smart person armed this way often ends up worse off than an ordinary one, because they can rationalize their way out of anything. The whole point of studying biases is to catch your own, and that is precisely the use we are most tempted to skip.

## Observance

Set a house rule for the week: you may only name a bias when it is running in your own head, never to win a point against someone else at the table. If you catch yourself reaching for the fancy vocabulary to dismiss another person, stop and ask what you would have to believe if they were right.

## Reading

> I've seen people severely messed up by their own knowledge of biases. They have more ammunition with which to argue against anything they don't like.
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Knowing About Biases Can Hurt People"
