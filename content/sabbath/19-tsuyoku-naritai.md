## Meaning

After a run of readings about the traps of the mind, this one turns to the mood you need to escape them: a cheerful, restless refusal to settle. Tsuyoku naritai is the cry of someone who has just lost, or just won, and still wants to be better tomorrow. It stands against the comfortable habit of declaring yourself already wise, already done, already good enough — the settledness that keeps people inside their old tribe long after they have outgrown it. The point is not self-punishment but forward motion: to treat every ceiling you reach as a floor. It is an aspiration you say out loud precisely because you have not yet arrived.

## Observance

Go around the table and have each person finish the sentence: this year I want to become stronger at ______. Keep it small and real. Say it as an aspiration, not a resolution to feel guilty about — the whole spirit is that wanting to grow is itself the good thing.

## Reading

> Tsuyoku is "strong"; naru is "becoming," and the form naritai is "want to become." Together it means "I want to become stronger," and it expresses a sentiment embodied more intensely in Japanese works than in any Western literature I've read.
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Tsuyoku Naritai! (I Want To Become Stronger)"
