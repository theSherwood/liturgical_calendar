## Meaning

Our moral intuitions were tuned for a small world of faces we know, and they buckle when asked to weigh large numbers against small probabilities. Yudkowsky lays out how this produces genuinely incoherent choices — preferring a sure small good to a slightly risky larger one in ways that would let a clever stranger pump you dry — and argues that when lives are at stake, the honest move is to do the arithmetic and follow it. The essay is the season's hard edge: the reminder that caring, done seriously, sometimes means overruling the very feelings that made you care.

## Observance

Read it together and let someone name the discomfort out loud — doing sums about lives does feel cold. Then talk about a real choice where the bigger number and the warmer feeling point in different directions. You do not have to resolve it tonight; the practice is just refusing to look away from the tension.

## Reading

> Does computing the expected utility feel too cold-blooded for your taste? Well, that feeling isn't even a feather in the scales, when a life is at stake. Just shut up and multiply.
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Circular Altruism"
