## Meaning

Here is a rule with real bite: you cannot arrange, in advance, for a test to confirm your belief no matter how it comes out. If one result would strengthen your view, some other possible result must weaken it — and the more likely the confirming result, the smaller the update it can give. This is why "heads I win, tails you lose" is not just unfair but literally impossible for honest evidence. Any experiment whose every outcome would leave you more sure is not an experiment at all. Held close, this idea makes it much harder to fool yourself.

## Observance

Read it together, then before checking some fact this week, say out loud what would make you believe it more and what would make you believe it less. If you cannot name the second, you are not really testing anything — notice that, and enjoy the catch.

## Reading

> If you expect a strong probability of seeing weak evidence in one direction, it must be balanced by a weak expectation of seeing strong evidence in the other direction.
> — Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Conservation of Expected Evidence"
