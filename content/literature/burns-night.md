## Meaning

Robert Burns was born on 25 January 1759, and Scots and their far-flung friends have marked the date with a supper ever since. Burns wrote in the everyday Scots of farmers and weavers rather than polite English, which is exactly why the poems still feel warm and human and slightly mischievous. The evening is gloriously silly and completely sincere at once: a plate of haggis is addressed as if it were a hero, whisky is raised, and poetry is spoken instead of merely read. It is proof that a language and a dead poet can still throw a good party. Few writers are loved so warmly by so many who have never studied them.

## Observance

Cook something hearty — haggis if you can get it, or a good stew if you can't — and read a Burns poem aloud before anyone eats. Raise a small toast, whisky for grown-ups and something else for everyone else. End, as the Scots do, with "Auld Lang Syne" and joined hands.

## Reading

> The best-laid schemes o' Mice an' Men
> Gang aft agley,
> An' lea'e us nought but grief an' pain,
> For promis'd joy!
> — Robert Burns, "To a Mouse" (1785)

Burns wrote it after turning up a mouse's nest with his plough — proof that even the smallest ruined plan is worth a poem.
