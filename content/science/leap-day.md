## Meaning

The Earth takes about 365.24 days to go once around the sun, and that stubborn quarter-day is why we have Leap Day. Left uncorrected, the calendar would drift until spring arrived in what we call December, so every four years we glue on a 29th of February to catch up. It is a small, elegant hack invented to keep human timekeeping aligned with the actual heavens. A day that exists only once every four years feels like found money — a rare extra square on the board of the year. It rewards anyone who enjoys the quiet machinery of the calendar.

## Observance

Treat the day as a genuine bonus and spend it deliberately: do the thing you always mean to and never get to. Explain to a child why February has an extra square this year and watch the idea land. Note what you did, so that four years from now you can compare.

## Reading

There is no ancient poem for a day the ancients kept losing. Its real text is arithmetic — 365.2422 days in a tropical year, reconciled by the rule that a leap year is any year divisible by four, except centuries not divisible by 400. That single clause, quietly correcting the sky, is one of the most successful pieces of engineering humans have ever written down.
