## Meaning

The birthday of the chemist whose exquisite X-ray photograph — the famous 'Photo 51' — revealed the double helix at the heart of every living thing. Watson and Crick used her data to win the race, and the Nobel, while her essential role was pushed to the footnotes; she died of cancer at 37, likely from her own radiation work, before the prize was even given. The day sets the record straight.

## Observance

Look at a picture of Photo 51 together — the blurry X that unlocked life. Talk about credit: who does the work, who gets the story, and how to make sure the two match. Honor the meticulous people whose careful data lets the famous leap.

## Reading

> You look at science (or at least talk of it) as some sort of demoralising invention of man, something apart from real life, and which must be cautiously guarded and kept separate from everyday existence. But science and everyday life cannot and should not be separated. Science, for me, gives a partial explanation of life. In so far as it goes, it is based on fact, experience and experiment. ... I maintain that faith in this world is perfectly possible without faith in another world.
> — Rosalind Franklin, letter to her father Ellis Franklin (summer 1940); quoted in Brenda Maddox, Rosalind Franklin: The Dark Lady of DNA (2002)
