## Meaning

The Emancipation Proclamation declared enslaved people in the rebelling states free on January 1, 1863, but a proclamation only becomes real where someone can enforce it. In Texas, the farthest corner of the Confederacy, slavery continued for another two and a half years. On June 19, 1865, Union General Gordon Granger reached Galveston and read out the order that freedom was now the law and would be enforced. Juneteenth marks that gap closing, the day the last held the same truth as the first, and it is now a United States federal holiday.

## Observance

Read General Order No. 3 aloud together, then talk about the distance between a right being declared and a right being real, and how long the news took to travel the last few hundred miles. Share a red food or drink, a Juneteenth tradition, and toast the people who had to wait longest for their freedom. It pairs naturally with the Emancipation Proclamation on New Year's Day, the promise and its keeping.

## Reading

> The people of Texas are informed that, in accordance with a proclamation from the Executive of the United States, all slaves are free. This involves an absolute equality of personal rights and rights of property between former masters and slaves, and the connection heretofore existing between them becomes that between employer and hired labor.
> — General Order No. 3, issued by Major General Gordon Granger, Galveston, Texas, June 19, 1865
