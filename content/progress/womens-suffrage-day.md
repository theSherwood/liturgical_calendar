## Meaning

On August 26, 1920, the Nineteenth Amendment was certified into the Constitution, and half the country that had been governed without its consent could finally vote. It was the end of a campaign that ran for more than seventy years, through petitions, marches, arrests, and hunger strikes, and it doubled the American electorate in a single sentence. The amendment did not instantly deliver the ballot to every woman, many Black women in particular were still blocked by other barriers for decades, but it settled the principle in the nation's founding document. Now observed as Women's Equality Day, it marks the moment the vote stopped depending on sex.

## Observance

Read the text of the Nineteenth Amendment out loud, and notice how short it is for how long it took. Talk about the suffragists who spent their whole lives on a right they might never use, and about how ordinary voting feels now because of them. If there is an election coming, make a plan together for who will vote and how.

## Reading

> The right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of sex.
> Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.
> — Nineteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, ratified 1920
