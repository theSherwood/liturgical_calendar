## Meaning

Nowruz means "new day," and it begins the year not on an arbitrary winter date but at the astronomical hinge of spring, when the sun crosses the equator and day and night stand equal. For some three thousand years, from Zoroastrian Persia onward, people have marked this exact moment as the world starting over. It is a new year you can actually feel: the light returns, the ground thaws, and the calendar agrees with the sky. Where most holidays ask you to imagine a fresh start, this one hands you a real one.

## Observance

In the days before the equinox, clean the house from top to bottom, the ritual "shaking of the house" that clears out the old year. Set a haft-sin table with seven symbolic things whose Persian names begin with the letter S, plus sprouted greens, a mirror, and something growing, and gather around it at the moment spring officially arrives. Wish each other a good year, and let the children hunt for whatever small treats you have hidden among the sprouts.

## Reading

Nowruz is older than any single poem about it, and its founding is told in Ferdowsi's Shahnameh, where the legendary king Jamshid rises on a jeweled throne on the first day of spring and the people, dazzled, name the day the New Day and keep it ever after. We could not confirm an exact translation of those lines against a reliable source, so rather than misquote a thousand-year-old poem we simply tell the story: the year, in this telling, begins with wonder.
